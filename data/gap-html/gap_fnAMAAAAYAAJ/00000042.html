One would imagine that Thucydides had done all that
could be done on this occasion, and deserved to be thanked
instead of punished. The people of Athens made a different
determination. Cleon was now the demagogue of greatest
influence there, and is generally supposed to have exasperated
them against the man, who had not wrought impossibilities
in saving their valuable town of Amphipolis. It is certain
their fury rose so high against him, that they stripped Thucy­
dides of his command, and passed the sentence of banish­
ment upon him. It is himself who tells usf, " It was his lot
" to suffer a twenty years exile from his country after the
" affair of Amphipolis."
