the lieutenant of the caliph intrusted the execution of
his own stratagem, which inclined the long-disputed
balance in favour of the Saracens. Supplying by ac­
tivity and artifice the deficiency of numbers, a part of
their forces lay concealed in their tents, while the re­
mainder prolonged an irregular skirmish with the enemy,
till the sun was high in the heavens. On both sides they
retired with fainting steps: their horses were unbridled,
their armour was laid aside, and the hostile nations pre­
pared, or seemed to prepare, for the refreshment of the
evening, and the encounter of the ensuing day. On a
sudden the charge was sounded; the Arabian camp
poured forth a swarm of fresh and intrepid warriors;
and the long line of the Greeks and Africans was sur­
prised, assaulted, overturned, by new squadrons of the
faithful, who, to the eye of fanaticism, might appear as
a band of angels descending from the sky. The prefect
himself was slain by the hand of Zobeir: his daughter,
who sought revenge and death, was surrounded and
made prisoner; and the fugitives involved in their
disaster the town of Sufetula, to which they escaped from
the sabres and lances of the Arabs. Sufetula was built
one hundred and fifty miles to the south of Carthage;
