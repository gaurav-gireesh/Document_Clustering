His first public oration was addressed to the Goths
and Romans, in the name of the emperor, who had
suspended for a while the conquest of Persia, and
listened to the prayers of his Italian subjects. He
gently touched on the causes and the authors of the
recent disasters; striving to remove the fear of pu­
nishment for the past, and the hope of impunity for
the future, and labouring, with more zeal than suc­
cess, to unite all the members of his government in
a firm league of affection and obedience. Justinian,
his gracious master, was inclined to pardon and re­
ward; and it was their interest, as well as duty, to
reclaim their deluded brethren, who had been se­
duced by the arts of the usurper. Not a man was
tempted to desert the standard of the Gothic king.
