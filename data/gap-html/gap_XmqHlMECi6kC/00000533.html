seldom worthy to reign; but the elevation of a private
man, of a peasant perhaps, or a slave, affords a strong
presumption of his courage and capacity. The viceroy
of a remote kingdom aspires to secure the property and
inheritance of his precarious trust; the nations must re­
joice in the presence of their sovereign; and the command
of armies and treasures are at once the object and the in­
strument of his ambition. A change was scarcely visible
as long as the lieutenants of the caliph were content with
their vicarious title; while they solicited for themselves
or their sons a renewal of the imperial grant, and still
maintained on the coin, and in the public prayers, the
name and prerogative of the commander of the faithful.
