who were contemporaries, added somewhat to the

expeditions sent out during the earlier emperors.
Thus, Pliny follows Agrippa in making India 3300

self suggests a different and shorter distance (vi.

contained 118 peoples and 60 rivers. The Emodus,
Imaus, Paropamisus, and Caucasus, he connected in

of these great mountains, the land was, like Egypt,
one vast plain (vi. 18. s. 22), comprehending many
wastes and much fruitful land (vi. 20. s. 23).
For a fuller notice of Taprobane than had been given
by previous writers, he was indebted to the ambas­

learnt that it had towards India a length of 10,000
stadia, and 500 towns,—one, the capital, Palaesi-
mundum, of vast size. The sea between it and the
continent is, he says, very shallow, and the distance
from the nearest point a journey of four days (vi. 22.
s. 24). The measurements of the distances round
the coast of India he gives with some minuteness,
and in some instances with less exaggeration than
his predecessors.
