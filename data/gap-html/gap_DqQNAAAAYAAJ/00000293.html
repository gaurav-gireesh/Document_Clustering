mere shadows of men, rendered lifeless by hunger,
cold, filth, and nastiness; battered and disabled among
the rocks and precipices. Add to this, their joints be­
numbed, their sinews stiffened, their limbs shrivelled
by the frost, their armor shattered and broken, their
horses lamed and enfeebled. Such is the infantry,
such the cavalry, with whom you are to fight. You
will have to deal, not with enemies, but the remains of
enemies. And nothing do I fear more than lest, before
you come to a battle, the Alps may appear to have
conquered Hannibal. But perhaps it was right that it
should be so; that, against a nation and commander
guilty of a breach of treaties, the gods themselves
should commence the war, and break the force of the
enemy; and that we who, next to' the gods, were the
party injured, should then take it up, and carry it on
to a conclusion.
