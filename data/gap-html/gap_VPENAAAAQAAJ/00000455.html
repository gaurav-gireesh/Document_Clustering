the Araxes. The great Pompey had formerly halted
within three days' march of the Caspian :* that inland sea
was explored for the first time, by a hostile fleet,' and
seventy thousand captives were transplanted from Hyr-
cania to the isle of Cyprus. On the return of spring,
Justinian descended into the fertile plains of Assyria, the
flames of war approached the residence of Nushirvan, the
indignant monarch sunk into the grave, and his last edict His death,
restrained his successors from exposing their person in a
battle against the Romans. Yet the memory of this
transient affront was lost in the glories of a long reign;
and his formidable enemies, after indulging their dream
of conquest, again solicited a short respite from the cala­
mities of war.8
