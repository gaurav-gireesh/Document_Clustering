put to sea was a plain proof that their intentions were in­
sincere throughout. Then they rifled their houses of all the
arms they could find ; and some of them who fell into their
hands had immediately been butchered if Nicostratus had not
interposed.

A second party, terrified at these proceedings, take their
seats also as suppliants in the temple of Juno. The num­
ber of these was not less than four hundred. The people,
grown now apprehensive of some fatal turn, persuade them
to leave their sanctuary ; and having prevailed, transport
them into that island which faceth the temple of Juno,
whither every thing needful for their sustenance was care­
fully sent them.

The sedition continuing in this posture, about the fourth or
fifth day after the transportation of the latter body into the
island, the Peloponnesian ships, which had assembled at Cyl-
lene after the voyage of Ionia, appear in sight to the number
of fifty-three. Alcidas was commander in chief as before,
and Brasidas attended as his council. They came to anchor
in the harbour of Sybota on the main ; and next morning, at
break of day, steered directly for Corcyra.
