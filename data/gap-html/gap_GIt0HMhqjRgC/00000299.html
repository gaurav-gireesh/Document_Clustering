Romans, the same spirit which had insulted, would
pursue the Avars to the extremity of the earth.
These specious reasons were heard by the chagan
with coldness and disdain: he detained the Lombard
ambassadors in his camp, protracted the negotiation,
and by turns alleged his want of inclination, or his
want of ability, to undertake this important enter­
prise. At length he signified the ultimate price of
his alliance, that the Lombards should immediately
present him with the tithe of their cattle; that the
spoils and captives should be equally divided; but
that the lands of the Gepidae should become the sole
patrimony of the Avars. Such hard conditions were
eagerly accepted by the passions of Alboin; and as
the Romans were dissatisfied with the ingratitude
and perfidy of the Gepidae, Justin abandoned that
