Want of arms I. It has been observed, with ingenuity, and not without truth,..

that the command of iron soon gives a nation the command of gold.
But the rude tribes of Germany, alike destitute of both those
valuable metals, were reduced slowly to acquire, by their unassisted
strength, the possession of the one as well as the other. The face
of a German army displayed their poverty of iron.. Swords,.and the
longer kind of lances, they could seldom use. Their frames- (as
they called them in their own language) were long spears headed
with a sharp but narrow iron point, and which, as occasion ver
quired, they either darted from a distance or pussied in close onset.
With this spear, and with a shield, their cavalry was contented. A
multitude of darts,, scattered 71 with incredible force were an addi­

wore any,, was nothing more than a loose mantle. A variety of
