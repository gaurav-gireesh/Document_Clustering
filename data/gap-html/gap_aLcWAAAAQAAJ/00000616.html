The vague descriptions of exile and imprisonment, of pain and
torture, are so easily exaggerated or softened by the pencil of an
artful orator, that we are naturally induced to inquire into a fact
of a more distinct and stubborn kind; the number of persons who
'suffered death in consequence of the edicts published by Diocletian,
his associates, and his successors. The recent legendaries record
whole armies and cities, which were at once swept away by the
undistinguishing rage of persecution. The more ancient writers content
themselves with pouring out a liberal effusion of loose and tragical in­
vectives, without condescending to ascertain the precise number of
those persons who were permitted to seal with their blood their belief
of the gospel. From the history of Eusebius, it may however be col­
lected, that only nine bishops were punished with death; and we are
