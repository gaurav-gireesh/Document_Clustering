27. Another war soon after arose with the Greeks of
the other coast. The Tarentines having for a consi­
derable time buoyed up the state of Palaepolis with
delusive hopes of assistance, when they understood
that the Romans had gotten possession of that city, as
if they were the persons who had suffered the disap­
pointment, and not the authors of it, they inveighed
against the Palaepolitans, and became furious in their
anger and malicje towards the Romans; to which they
were farther incited by receiving information that the
Lucanians and Apulians had joined them ; for a treaty
of alliance had been this year concluded with both
these nations. ' The business,' they observed, ' was
now brought almost to their doors ; and such wonld
soon be the state of affairs, that they must deal with
the Romans as enemies, or receive them as masters :
that, in fact, their interests were at stake on the issue
of the war of the Samnites, the only nation which con­
tinued to make opposition ; and that with power very
inadequate since they were deserted by the Lucanians ;
these however might yet be brought back, and induced to
renounce the Roman alliance, if proper skill were used
in sowing dissension between them.' These reasonings
being readily adopted by people who wished for a
change, they procured, for money, some young Luca­
nians of considerable note in their country, but devoid
of honor, to bring about their design: these having
lacerated each other's bodies with stripes, came naked
into a public meeting of their countrymen, exclaiming
that because they had ventured to go into the Roman
camp, they had been thus beaten with rods by order of
the consul, and had hardly escaped the loss of their
