could not save the unhappy general from their licen­

officers, were inhumanly murdered: their mangled
bodies were dragged about the streets; and the em­
peror, who then resided at Milan, was surprised by
the intelligence of the audacious and wanton cruelty
of the people of Thessalonica. The sentence of a
dispassionate judge would have inflicted a severe pu­
nishment on the authors of the crime; and the merit
of Botheric might contribute to exasperate the grief
and indignation of his master. The fiery and choleric
temper of Theodosius was impatient of the dilatory
forms of a judicial inquiry; and he hastily resolved,
that the blood of his lieutenant should be expiated
by the blood of the guilty people. Yet his mind still
