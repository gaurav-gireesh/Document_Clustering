not a melancholy truth, that the first and most cruel suf­
ferings must be the lot of the innocent and helpless, his­
tory might exult in the misery of the conquerors, who,
in the midst of riches, were left destitute of bread or
wine, reduced to drink the waters of the Po, and to feed
on the flesh of distempered cattle. The dysentery swept
away one-third of their army; and the clamours of his
subjects, who were impatient to pass the Alps, disposed
Theodebert to listen with respect to the mild exhorta­
tions of Belisarius. The memory of this inglorious and
destructive warfare was perpetuated on the medals of
Gaul: and Justinian, without unsheathing his sword,
assumed the title of conqueror of the Franks. The
Merovingian prince was offended by the vanity of the
emperor; he affected to pity the fallen fortunes of the
Goths; and his insidious offer of a federal union was
fortified by the promise or menace of descending from
the Alps at the head of five hundred thousand men. His
plans of conquest were boundless, and perhaps chimerical.
