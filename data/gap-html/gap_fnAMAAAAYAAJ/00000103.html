Such advice was now given to the people of Syracuse by
Hermocrates. , That community, it is evident, was full of
cabal and faction, since this worthy patriot was regarded as
a party-tool and a public incendiary. Athenagoras, the blus­
tering demagogue who replies, treats him in this light. His
virulence shews, that he regarded Hermocrates, as one who
wanted by any means whatever to force himself into employ­
ment. He seems more alarmed for the lucrative posts of the
State than for the welfare of his country. He throws out a
deal of good-sense, but in a very impertinent and scurrilous
manner. Such are the persons, who study popularity more
than duty, and sacrifice all their talents to ambition or private
lucre.
