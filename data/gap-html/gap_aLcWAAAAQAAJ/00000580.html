The exiled bishop enjoyed the conveniencies of life and the con­
sciousness of virtue. His reputation was diffused over Africa and
Italy; an account of his behaviour was publistied for the edification
of the Christian world 81; and his solitude was frequently interrupted
by the letters, the visits, and the congratulations of the faithful.
On the arrival of a new proconsul in the province, the fortune of
Cyprian appeared for some time to wear a still more favourable as­
pect. He was recalled from banishment; and though not yet per­
mitted to return to Carthage, his own gardens in the neighbourhood
of the capital were assigned for the place of his residence
