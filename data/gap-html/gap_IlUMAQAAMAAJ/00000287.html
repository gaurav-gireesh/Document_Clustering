the desolation of the adjacent country, Julian cast
many an anxious look towards the North, in full ex­
pectation, that as hehimself had victoriously penetrated
to the capital of Sapor, the march and junction of his
lieutenants, Sebastian and Procopius, would be exe­
cuted with the same courage and diligence. His ex­
pectations were disappointed by the treachery of the
Armenian king, who permitted, and most probably
directed, the desertion of his auxiliary troops from
the camp of the Romans f; and by the dissensions
of the two generals, who were incapable of forming
or executing any plan for the public service. When
the emperor had relinquished the hope of this im­
portant reinforcement, he condescended to hold a
council of war, and approved, after a full debate, the
sentiment of those generals, who dissuaded the siege of
Ctesiphon, as a fruitless and pernicious undertaking.
