had shared one thousand, and each horseman three thou­
sand, pieces, in the real division of the plunder. The au­
thor of the death of Gregory was expected to have claim­
ed the most precious reward of the victory: from his
silence it might be presumed that he had fallen in the
battle, till the tears and exclamations of the prefect's
daughter at the sight of Zobeir revealed the valour and
modesty of that gallant soldier. The unfortunate virgin
was offered, and almost rejected, as a slave, by her fa­
ther's murderer, who coolly declared that his sword was
consecrated to the service of religion; and that he la­
boured for a recompense far above the charms of mortal
beauty, or the riches of this transitory life. A reward
congenial to his temper, was the honourable commission
of announcing to the caliph Othman the success of his
arms. The companions, the chiefs, and the people, were
