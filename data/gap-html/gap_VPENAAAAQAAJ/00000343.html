pontiffs and the patricians. Like the Chaldean astrolo­
gers, they announced to their clients the days of busi­
ness and repose; these important trifles were interwoven
with the religion of Numa; and, after the publication of
the twelve tables, the Roman people was still enslaved by
the ignorance of judicial proceedings. The treachery of
some plebeian officers at length revealed the profitable
mystery: in a more enlightened age, the legal actions
were derided and observed; and the same antiquity
which sanctified the practice, obliterated the use and
