48. A great part of the senate, induced by their re­
gard for him, and the importance of his services,
showed an inclination to grant his request. The elder
part refused to agree to such grant, both 'because the
army, with which he had acted, belonged to another;
and because he had left his province through an am­
bitious desire of snatching that opportunity of pro­
curing a triumph, — a conduct altogether unprece­
dented.' The senators of consular rank particularly
insisted, that ' he ought to have waited for the con­
sul; for that he might, by pitching his camp near the
city, and thereby securing the colony without coming
to an engagement, have protracted the affair until his
arrival; and that, what the pretor had not done, the
senate ought to do; they should wait for the consul.
After hearing the business discussed by the consul and
pretor in their presence, they would be able, on better
grounds, to form a judgment on the case.' Great part
