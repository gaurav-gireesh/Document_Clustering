motions on the side of Gaul, nor join his forces to
Hannibal's: and Hannibal himself was now declining
both in reputation and strength.' Their minds, which,
in consequence of the late news, had sunk into these
desponding reflections, were again recalled, by dread
of the impending evils, to consult how they might op­
pose the present dangers. They resolved to levy
soldiers with all haste, both in the city and the coun­
try; to hire auxiliaries from the Africans ; to strengthen
the forts; to collect oorn; to prepare weapons and ar­
mor; to fit out ships, and send them to Hippo against the
Roman fleet. While they were thus employed, news at
length arrived that it was Laelius, and not Scipio, who
had come over; that his forces were no more than what
were sufficient to make plundering incursions; and that
the main force of the enemy was still in Sicily. Thus
they got time to breathe, and began to despatch em­
