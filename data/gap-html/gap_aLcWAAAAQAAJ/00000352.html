It was observed by one of the most sagacious of the Roman
princes, that the talents of his predecessor Aurelian, were better
suited to the command of an army, than to the government of an
empire95. Conscious of the character in which Nature and expe­
rience had enabled him to excel, he again took the field a few
months after his triumph. It was expedient to exercise the restless
temper of the legions in some foreign war, and the Persian monarch,
exulting in the shame of Valerian, still braved with impunity the
offended majesty of Rome. At the head of an army, less formidable
by its numbers than by its discipline and valour, the emperor ad­
