and hospitable. As their supreme god, they adored
an invisible master of the thunder. The rivers and
the nymphs obtained their subordinate honours, and
the popular worship was expressed in vows and sa­
crifice. The Sclavonians disdained to obey a despot,
a prince, or even a magistrate; but their experience
was too narrow, their passions too headstrong, to
compose a system of equal law or general defence.
Some voluntary respect was yielded to age and va­
lour; but each tribe or village existed as a separate
republic, and all must be persuaded where none could
be compelled. They fought on foot, almost naked,
and, except an unwieldy shield, without any defensive
armour: their weapons of offence were a bow, a
quiver of small poisoned arrows, and a long rope,
which they dexterously threw from a distance, and
entangled their enemy in a running noose. In the
field, the Sclavonian infantry was dangerous by their
speed, agility, and hardiness : they swam, they dived,
they remained under water, drawing their breath
through a hollow cane; and a river or lake was often
the scene of their unsuspected ambuscade. But
these were the achievements of spies or stragglers;
the military art was unknown to the Sclavonians;
their name was obscure, and their conquests were
inglorious.
