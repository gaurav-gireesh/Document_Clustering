restored the liberty of the schools, and were convinced, by the experience of ages,
that the moral character of philosophers is not affected by the diversity of their
theological speculations. (5)

The Gothic arms were less fatal to the schools of Athens than the establishment
of a new religion, whose ministers superseded the exercise of reason, resolved eve­
ry question by an article of faith, and condemned the infidel or sceptic to eternal
flames. In many a volume of laborious controversy, they exposed the weakness
of the understanding and the corruption of the heart, insulted human nature in the
sages of antiquity, and proscribed the spirit of philosophical inquiry, so repugnant
to the doctrine, or at least to the temper, of an humble believer. The surviving
sect of the Platonists, whom Plato would have blushed to acknowledge, extrava­
gantly mingled a sublime theory with the practice of superstition and magic; and,
as they remained alone in the midst of a Christian world, they indulged a secret
rancour against the government of the church and state; whose severity was still
suspended over their heads. About a century after the reign of Julian, (6) Pro­
