were unanimously acknowledged as Roman emperors. The public
expected that the successor of Carus would pursue his father's foot­
steps, and, without allowing the Persians to recover from their con­
sternation, would advance sword in hand to the palaces of Susa and
Ecbatana 11. But the legions, however strong in numbers and dis­
cipline, were dismayed by the most abject superstition. Notwith­
standing all the arts that were practised to disguise the manner of the
late emperor's death, it was found impossible to remove the opinion
of the multitude, and the power of opinion is irresistible. Places or
persons struck with lightning were considered by the ancients with
pious horror, as singularly devoted to the wrath of Heaven 78. An
oracle was remembered, which marked the river Tigris as the
fatal boundary of the Roman arms. The troops, terrified with the
fate of Carus and with their own danger, called aloud on young
Numerian to obey the will of the gods, and to lead them away
from this inauspicious scene of war. The feeble emperor was un­
able to subdue their obstinate prejudice, and the Persians wondered
at the unexpected retreat of a victorious enemy 7'.
